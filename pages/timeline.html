Timeline of the Pacific Northwestern Railroads

  • 1850: The Oregon Donation Land Act is passed by the United States Congress, encouraging settlement in the Oregon Territory by offering land grants.
    The first steam-powered vessel, the "SS Beaver," arrives in Puget Sound, demonstrating the potential for steam-powered transportation in the Pacific Northwest.
  • 1851: The first steam-powered sawmill in the Pacific Northwest is established near Astoria, Oregon, demonstrating the region's industrial potential and increasing the demand for transportation infrastructure.
    The Treaty of Fort Laramie establishes the Oregon Trail as a legitimate route for emigrants, contributing to the influx of settlers into the Pacific Northwest.
  • 1852: The first steamboat, the "Lot Whitcomb," arrives in the Willamette River, revolutionizing transportation in the region and facilitating trade and commerce.
    The first newspaper in the Oregon Territory, the "Oregon Spectator," begins publication, helping to disseminate information about transportation developments and promote settlement.
  • 1853: The United States Congress establishes the Washington Territory, recognizing the growing population and need for governance in the region.
    The first telegraph line is established in the Pacific Northwest, connecting Portland, Oregon, with the East Coast, improving communication and facilitating economic development.
  • 1854: The Northern Pacific Railroad Company is incorporated, signaling early efforts to establish railway connections in the Pacific Northwest.
    The first public school in the Oregon Territory is established in Portland, laying the foundation for education and human capital development in the region.
  • 1855: The Yakima War erupts in the Washington Territory, highlighting the need for improved transportation infrastructure to facilitate military movements and supply lines.
    The first lighthouse in the Pacific Northwest, the Cape Disappointment Lighthouse, becomes operational, aiding navigation along the treacherous coastal waters and promoting maritime trade.
  • 1856: The Puget Sound War erupts in the Washington Territory, underscoring the need for secure transportation routes and communication networks to support military operations and civilian settlements.
    The first stagecoach service begins operation between Portland, Oregon, and the Willamette Valley, improving land transportation options and facilitating trade and travel.
  • 1857: The Oregon Territorial Legislature charters the Willamette Valley and Cascade Mountain Wagon Road Company, aiming to improve transportation between the Willamette Valley and Eastern Oregon.
    The Fraser Canyon Gold Rush in British Columbia spurs increased traffic and commerce along the Columbia River, highlighting the importance of water transportation in the region.
  • 1858: Gold is discovered along the Fraser River in British Columbia, leading to the Fraser River Gold Rush and further stimulating economic activity and population growth in the Pacific Northwest.
    The Oregon Steam Navigation Company is established, consolidating steamboat operations along the Columbia River and contributing to the development of a regional transportation network.
  • 1859: Oregon achieves statehood, giving it greater autonomy and influence in advocating for transportation and infrastructure projects.
    The first transcontinental mail service is established, connecting the Pacific Northwest with the eastern United States and improving communication and commerce.
  • 1860: The Pony Express begins operation, providing a faster mail service between the East Coast and the Pacific Northwest, albeit temporarily.
    The Overland Stage Company initiates stagecoach service between Walla Walla, Washington Territory, and Salt Lake City, Utah, improving overland transportation options.
  • 1861: The American Civil War begins, diverting attention and resources away from westward expansion and potentially slowing down railway development in the Pacific Northwest.
    President Abraham Lincoln signs the Pacific Railway Act, authorizing the construction of the transcontinental railroad, which would eventually connect the Pacific Northwest with the rest of the country, signaling the federal government's commitment to western expansion.
  • 1862: The Homestead Act is passed by Congress, offering 160 acres of public land to settlers in exchange for five years of residency and land improvement, further incentivizing settlement and the need for transportation infrastructure.
    The Union Pacific Railroad Company is chartered by Congress to build the eastern portion of the transcontinental railroad, setting the stage for future railway development in the Pacific Northwest.
  • 1863: Construction of the Oregon Central Military Wagon Road begins, aiming to improve transportation between the Willamette Valley and Eastern Oregon, particularly for military purposes during the Civil War.
    The Idaho Territory is established by Congress, increasing the focus on transportation connections to the Pacific Coast and stimulating discussions about railway development.
  • 1864: Completion of the First Transcontinental Telegraph connects the Pacific Northwest with the East Coast, revolutionizing communication in the region.
    The Oregon and California Railroad Company is incorporated, with plans to construct a railway line from Portland, Oregon, to California, further promoting regional connectivity and economic development.
  • 1865: The American Civil War ends with the surrender of the Confederate forces, allowing resources and attention to be redirected towards westward expansion and railway development in the Pacific Northwest.
    The Oregon State Legislature grants a charter to the Oregon Central Railroad Company, paving the way for the construction of a railway line from Portland to the Willamette Valley and beyond, enhancing transportation options in the region.
  • 1866: The Oregon Steam Navigation Company expands its operations, dominating steamboat transportation along the Columbia River and contributing to regional economic growth.
    The Puget Sound region experiences a population boom due to increased immigration and settlement, driving demand for improved transportation infrastructure.
  • 1867: Construction of the Northern Pacific Railway begins in Minnesota, with plans to extend the line to the Pacific Northwest, sparking excitement and anticipation for enhanced railway connectivity in the region.
    The British North America Act establishes the Dominion of Canada, setting the stage for increased cooperation and development of transportation links between British Columbia and the Pacific Northwest.
  • 1868: Completion of the Union Pacific Railroad to Promontory Summit, Utah, marks a major milestone in transcontinental railway construction, bringing the Pacific Northwest closer to a coast-to-coast connection.
    The Oregon and California Railroad reaches Roseburg, Oregon, advancing the construction of a railway line linking Portland with California and promoting economic integration within the Pacific Northwest.
  • 1869: The First Transcontinental Railroad is completed with the driving of the Golden Spike at Promontory Summit, Utah, establishing a direct railway connection between the Pacific Northwest and the eastern United States, revolutionizing transportation and commerce.
    The Oregon State Legislature approves the Oregon and California Railroad Land Grant, providing land to support the construction of a railway line from Portland to California, facilitating further economic development and settlement in the region.
  • 1870: The Oregon and California Railroad completes its line to the Oregon-California border, further solidifying transportation connections between the Pacific Northwest and California.
    The Northern Pacific Railway reaches Fargo, Dakota Territory, continuing its westward expansion and bringing the Pacific Northwest closer to a transcontinental railway connection with the Great Lakes region and the East Coast.
  • 1871: The Oregon and California Railroad completes its line to Ashland, Oregon, further extending railway access into Southern Oregon and facilitating trade and settlement in the region.
    The Northern Pacific Railway reaches Bismarck, Dakota Territory, marking significant progress in its westward expansion and bringing the Pacific Northwest closer to a transcontinental railway connection.
  • 1872: The Northern Pacific Railway establishes Tacoma, Washington, as its western terminus on Puget Sound, stimulating economic development and urban growth in the region.
    The Oregon and California Railroad completes its line to California's border, solidifying railway connections between the Pacific Northwest and California and facilitating interstate commerce.
  • 1873: The Northern Pacific Railway reaches Portland, Oregon, completing its transcontinental route and providing direct rail access to the Pacific Northwest's largest city and port.
    The financial Panic of 1873 slows railway construction and economic development in the Pacific Northwest, leading to financial challenges for railway companies and delaying further expansion.
  • 1874: The Oregon and California Railroad extends its line southward into California's Central Valley, enhancing transportation links between the Pacific Northwest and California's agricultural heartland.
    The Northern Pacific Railway begins regular passenger and freight service between Portland and points east, bolstering trade and commerce in the Pacific Northwest.
  • 1875: The Northern Pacific Railway completes construction of its branch line from Kalama, Washington, to Tacoma, further integrating the Puget Sound region into the transcontinental railway network.
    The Oregon Central Railroad is completed, connecting Portland with the Willamette Valley and facilitating the movement of goods and passengers between the city and its agricultural hinterland.
  • 1876: The Seattle and Walla Walla Railroad is established, aiming to connect the Puget Sound region with southeastern Washington, although construction delays hinder progress.
    The Oregon and California Railroad completes its line to Ashland, Oregon, further extending railway access into Southern Oregon and facilitating trade and settlement in the region.
  • 1877: The completion of the Seattle, Lake Shore and Eastern Railway's line to Newcastle, Washington, facilitates the transportation of coal from the Coal Creek mines to the Seattle area.
    The Oregon Railway and Navigation Company is formed, consolidating several smaller railway lines in the Pacific Northwest and aiming to improve transportation efficiency and connectivity.
  • 1878: The Oregon and California Railroad extends its line southward into California's Central Valley, enhancing transportation links between the Pacific Northwest and California's agricultural heartland.
    The Seattle, Lake Shore and Eastern Railway extends its line to Renton, Washington, further supporting the coal mining industry and industrial development in the Puget Sound region.
  • 1879: The Oregon Railway and Navigation Company completes its line from Portland to Wallula, Washington, improving transportation access along the Columbia River and facilitating trade with the interior regions.
    The Northern Pacific Railway completes construction of its line to Tacoma, Washington, further solidifying the city's importance as a major transportation hub in the Pacific Northwest.
  • 1880: The Oregon Railway and Navigation Company expands its network with the completion of a line from Wallula, Washington, to Walla Walla, improving transportation access to southeastern Washington.
    The Seattle, Lake Shore and Eastern Railway extends its line to Snoqualmie Falls, Washington, facilitating the development of hydroelectric power and supporting industrial growth in the region.
  • 1881: The Seattle, Lake Shore and Eastern Railway completes its line to the town of Issaquah, Washington, supporting the logging and mining industries in the region.
    The Oregon and California Railroad completes its line to the California border, further solidifying railway connections between the Pacific Northwest and California.
  • 1882: The Oregon Railway and Navigation Company completes its line from Walla Walla, Washington, to Pendleton, Oregon, improving transportation access to the interior regions of Eastern Oregon.
    The Spokane Falls and Northern Railway is incorporated, aiming to connect Spokane, Washington, with mining and timber resources in the surrounding areas.
  • 1883: The Oregon Railway and Navigation Company extends its line to the Idaho border, enhancing transportation links between the Pacific Northwest and the inland regions.
    The Seattle, Lake Shore and Eastern Railway completes its line to the coal mines at Gilman (now Issaquah), Washington, further supporting the coal mining industry in the region.
  • 1884: The Oregon Short Line Railroad, a subsidiary of the Union Pacific Railroad, is completed, providing a direct rail link between Oregon and the Union Pacific mainline at Granger, Wyoming.
    The Spokane Falls and Northern Railway completes its line to the town of Cheney, Washington, enhancing transportation access to agricultural and timber resources in the region.
  • 1885: The Oregon Railway and Navigation Company completes its line to Huntington, Oregon, on the Snake River, improving transportation access to eastern Oregon and facilitating trade with Idaho.
    The Spokane, Portland and Seattle Railway is incorporated, aiming to connect Spokane, Washington, with Portland, Oregon, via the Columbia River Gorge, although construction does not begin until later.
  • 1886: The Seattle, Lake Shore and Eastern Railway extends its line to the town of North Bend, Washington, supporting timber harvesting and providing access to the growing community.
    The Oregon Railway and Navigation Company completes its line to the Dalles, Oregon, further improving transportation access along the Columbia River.
  • 1887: The Spokane, Portland and Seattle Railway begins construction on its line from Spokane, Washington, to Portland, Oregon, aiming to improve transportation links between the two cities.
    The Great Northern Railway extends its line into Washington Territory, reaching Spokane Falls and connecting the city with the transcontinental railway network.
  • 1888: The Oregon Short Line Railroad completes its line to the town of Huntington, Oregon, providing a direct rail link between Oregon and the Union Pacific mainline at Granger, Wyoming.
    The Seattle, Lake Shore and Eastern Railway expands its line to the town of Cedar Falls, Washington, supporting timber logging and providing access to natural resources in the area.
  • 1889: The Spokane, Portland and Seattle Railway completes its line from Spokane, Washington, to Pasco, Washington, improving transportation access in the Columbia Basin region.
    The Great Northern Railway completes construction of its Cascade Tunnel through the Cascade Mountains, providing a more direct and efficient route for rail traffic between the eastern and western parts of Washington State.
  • 1890: The Seattle, Lake Shore and Eastern Railway extends its line to the town of Snoqualmie, Washington, supporting the timber and logging industries in the area.
    The Oregon Railway and Navigation Company completes its line to the city of Spokane, Washington, improving transportation access to and from the Inland Northwest region.
    The Spokane, Portland and Seattle Railway completes its line from Pasco, Washington, to Vancouver, Washington, providing a direct rail connection between Spokane and Portland.
    The Great Northern Railway extends its line into Seattle, Washington, establishing the city as a major hub for railway transportation in the Pacific Northwest.
  • 1891: The Oregon Short Line Railroad completes construction of its line from Huntington, Oregon, to the town of Ontario, Oregon, improving transportation access in eastern Oregon.
    The Seattle, Lake Shore and Eastern Railway extends its line to the town of Preston, Washington, supporting agricultural development in the Snoqualmie Valley.
  • 1892: The Great Northern Railway completes construction of its line from Seattle, Washington, to Everett, Washington, facilitating trade and passenger travel between the two cities.
    The Spokane, Portland and Seattle Railway extends its line to the city of Portland, Oregon, further enhancing transportation connectivity in the region.
