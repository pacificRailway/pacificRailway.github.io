Timeline of the Pacific Northwestern Railroads

  • 1850: Donation Land Claim Act encourages settlement in Oregon Territory, indirectly boosting future rail development.
  • 1851: Founding of Seattle, Washington, sets stage for future rail connections to major ports.
  • 1852: Construction of the first wagon road over the Cascades, the Naches Pass Trail, highlights the need for better transportation routes.
  • 1853: Northern Pacific Railroad survey explores routes through the Cascade Mountains.
  • 1854: Completion of the Oregon Trail increases pressure for efficient transportation networks, including railroads.
  • 1855: Washington Territory is created, emphasizing the need for regional infrastructure development.
  • 1856: First legislative support for railroads in Washington Territory.
  • 1857: Oregon Constitutional Convention discusses transportation improvements, including railroads.
  • 1858: Fraser River Gold Rush in British Columbia drives demand for better transportation, including railroads.
  • 1859: Oregon achieves statehood, promoting economic development and future rail projects.
  • 1860: Discovery of gold in Idaho accelerates demand for transportation networks, including railroads.
  • 1861: First transcontinental telegraph line completed, laying groundwork for rail communication networks.
  • 1862: Pacific Railway Act signed, authorizing the construction of the first transcontinental railroad.
  • 1863: Construction begins on the Central Pacific Railroad from California, aiming to connect with the Union Pacific.
  • 1864: Northern Pacific Railroad is chartered by Congress to build a rail line from Lake Superior to the Pacific Coast.
  • 1865: Oregon Steam Navigation Company (OSN) established, dominating regional transportation.
  • 1866: OSN's rail line between The Dalles and Celilo Falls on the Columbia River begins operation.
  • 1867: Coal discovered near Tacoma, Washington, promoting future rail connections to the area.
  • 1868: San Francisco & Humboldt Railroad incorporated to serve Northern California.
  • 1869: First Transcontinental Railroad completed, boosting western rail expansion.
  • 1870: Oregon & California Railroad reaches Roseburg, Oregon, facilitating regional trade.
  • 1871: Northern Pacific Railroad construction begins near Kalama, Washington.
  • 1872: Great Northern Railway begins surveys for a northern transcontinental route.
  • 1873: Northern Pacific reaches Tacoma, Washington, establishing a critical Pacific port.
  • 1874: Oregon Steam Navigation Company merged into Oregon Steamship Company, consolidating regional transport.
  • 1875: Villard’s German investors fund the Oregon Railway and Navigation Company (OR&N), which later acquires OSN.
  • 1876: Oregonian Railway Company begins construction of narrow-gauge lines in the Willamette Valley.
  • 1877: The Nez Perce War in Idaho and Oregon disrupts rail and other transportation plans.
  • 1878: Northern Pacific reaches Billings, Montana, on its route to the Pacific Northwest.
  • 1879: Oregonian Railway’s narrow-gauge line reaches Dayton, Oregon.
  • 1880: Oregon Short Line Railroad begins operation in Idaho, connecting to the Union Pacific.
  • 1881: Oregon Railway and Navigation Company (OR&N) completes line to Portland, Oregon.
  • 1882: Northern Pacific Railroad completes its bridge over the Missouri River at Bismarck.
  • 1883: Northern Pacific completes its transcontinental line, reaching Tacoma, Washington.
  • 1884: Canadian Pacific Railway reaches the Pacific at Port Moody, British Columbia.
  • 1885: Canadian Pacific Railway completed, connecting British Columbia to Eastern Canada.
  • 1886: Great Northern Railway reaches Spokane, Washington.
  • 1887: Southern Pacific’s Shasta Route reaches Portland, Oregon.
  • 1888: Union Pacific acquires control of the OR&N, consolidating its presence in the region.
  • 1889: Washington achieves statehood, fostering regional rail development.
  • 1890: Northern Pacific builds a branch line to Seattle, Washington.
  • 1891: Great Northern Railway reaches Everett, Washington, and later extends to Seattle.
  • 1892: Spokane, Portland & Seattle Railway (SP&S) is incorporated.
  • 1893: Financial Panic of 1893 leads to bankruptcy of several major railroads, including the Northern Pacific.
  • 1894: Great Northern Railway completes its route to Seattle, Washington.
  • 1895: Northern Pacific reorganized under the control of J.P. Morgan.
  • 1896: Klondike Gold Rush spurs demand for rail transport in the Pacific Northwest.
  • 1897: White Pass and Yukon Route (WP&YR) is planned to connect Skagway, Alaska, with the Yukon.
  • 1898: SP&S Railway begins construction along the north bank of the Columbia River.
  • 1899: Coeur d'Alene Railway and Navigation Company links mines in Idaho to rail networks.
  • 1900: Northern Pacific completes a major branch line to the Puget Sound area.
  • 1901: Hill-Harriman feud begins over control of Northern Pacific.
  • 1902: New York Stock Exchange panic as Hill and Harriman battle for control of Northern Pacific stock.
  • 1903: Milwaukee Road (Chicago, Milwaukee, St. Paul & Pacific Railroad) announces its Pacific Extension.
  • 1904: Milwaukee Road begins construction of its Pacific Extension.
  • 1905: Oregon Electric Railway opens a line from Portland to Salem, Oregon.
  • 1906: San Francisco earthquake and fire disrupt regional rail operations.
  • 1907: Milwaukee Road reaches the Missouri River in South Dakota.
  • 1908: Chicago, Milwaukee, St. Paul & Pacific (Milwaukee Road) completes its Pacific Extension to Seattle.
  • 1909: Alaska-Yukon-Pacific Exposition held in Seattle, promoting regional rail connections.
  • 1910: Glacier National Park established, with Great Northern Railway providing access.
  • 1911: Western Pacific Railroad completes its Feather River route to Northern California.
  • 1912: Southern Pacific completes its Natron Cutoff in Oregon.
  • 1913: Pacific Great Eastern Railway (later BC Rail) incorporated in British Columbia.
  • 1914: First through passenger train runs from Chicago to Seattle via Milwaukee Road.
  • 1915: Panama-Pacific International Exposition in San Francisco highlights rail access to the Pacific Northwest.
  • 1916: Last spike driven on the Pacific Great Eastern Railway in British Columbia.
  • 1917: United States enters World War I; railroads placed under federal control.
  • 1918: Spokane, Portland & Seattle Railway completes line to Bend, Oregon.
  • 1919: Railroads returned to private ownership after federal control during WWI.
  • 1920: Canadian National Railway formed, consolidating several regional railways in British Columbia.
  • 1921: Northern Pacific Railway completes electrification project in the Cascade Tunnel.
  • 1922: Spokane International Railroad connects to Canadian Pacific Railway at Kingsgate, British Columbia.
  • 1923: Construction of the Okanagan Valley Railway in British Columbia.
  • 1924: Electrification of the Milwaukee Road's Pacific Extension in Montana and Idaho begins.
  • 1925: Canadian Pacific Railway completes the Kettle Valley Railway in British Columbia.
  • 1926: Burlington Northern and Santa Fe Railway (BNSF) predecessor companies expand in the Pacific Northwest.
  • 1927: Milwaukee Road completes electrification from Othello, Washington, to Tacoma.
  • 1928: Great Northern Railway completes its Cascade Tunnel in Washington.
  • 1929: Stock market crash leads to economic downturn, affecting railroad revenues.
  • 1930: Milwaukee Road completes electrification of its entire Pacific Extension.
  • 1931: Northern Pacific completes its second Cascade Tunnel, the longest in North America.
  • 1932: Great Depression impacts rail traffic and financial stability of many railroads.
  • 1933: Federal New Deal programs support infrastructure improvements, including railroads.
  • 1934: Union Pacific introduces the streamlined "City of Portland" passenger train.
  • 1935: Federal regulation of railroads increased through the Interstate Commerce Act.
  • 1936: Southern Pacific introduces the "Cascade" passenger train service.
  • 1937: Streamlined passenger trains become more common on Pacific Northwest routes.
  • 1938: Northern Pacific introduces the "North Coast Limited" streamlined train.
  • 1939: World War II preparations boost rail traffic in the Pacific Northwest.
  • 1940: Completion of the Grand Coulee Dam increases demand for rail transport of materials.
  • 1941: United States enters World War II, leading to increased military rail traffic.
  • 1942: Construction of the Alaska Highway boosts rail connections to the Pacific Northwest.
  • 1943: Railroads play a crucial role in transporting war materials and troops during WWII.
  • 1944: Post-war planning begins for modernization of rail infrastructure.
  • 1945: End of World War II sees transition to peacetime rail operations.
  • 1946: Passenger rail travel peaks in the post-war economic boom.
  • 1947: Diesel locomotives begin to replace steam engines on many lines.
  • 1948: Columbia Basin Project brings agricultural development, increasing rail freight traffic.
  • 1949: Streamlined diesel passenger trains introduced on many Pacific Northwest routes.
  • 1950: Introduction of containerized freight starts to transform rail logistics.
